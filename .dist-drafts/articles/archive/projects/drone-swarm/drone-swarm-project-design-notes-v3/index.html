Drone Swarm Project — Design Notes v3

2026-04-01
archiveimported

Overview#

A multi-functional drone swarm system built around two drone types:

  • Async drones — full sensor suite, independent AI capable, worn on a belt, used for personal recon, surveillance, podcasting, forensics, dark environments, pointing/scanning tasks, and WiFi through-wall sensing.
  • Sync drones — stripped-down vision-first design, centrally coordinated, stored in a suitcase, used for formations, light shows, multi-angle filming, perimeter coverage, group recon, and distributed WiFi sensing meshes.

Both fleets connect to a central computer backpack worn by the operator. A head-mounted display (HMD) shows drone feeds and controls. Motion gloves allow precision manual control of any individual drone. Solar panels on the suitcase, backpack, and belt extend field endurance without access to shore power.

Drone Types#

Async Drones (Belt, 4 units)#

Full-featured drones for individual, versatile tasks. Each one is capable of acting independently.

Capabilities:

  • Day/night dual-mode camera (switchable IR-cut filter)
  • Optional thermal camera for heat detection, dark recon, forensics
  • IR LED illumination for night vision (850 nm)
  • UV LED for forensic scanning (395 nm)
  • White LED flashlight for caves, woods, dark environments
  • GPS for precise positioning, last-known location, and pointing triangulation
  • Time-of-flight sensor (VL53L5CX) for obstacle avoidance and gesture triangulation
  • LoRa communication with backpack
  • Voice-command activated via backpack microphone array
  • Gesture control via motion gloves (precision mode)
  • Biometric security (fingerprint, best implemented at belt/backpack level)
  • Optional SDR for passive RF listening (receive-only — see legal notes)
  • WiFi CSI sensing (ESP32-S3) for through-wall human detection — see WiFi Sensing section
  • Surveillance AI — face detection, person tracking, license plate recognition, geo-tagged evidence — see Surveillance section

Palm Auto-Return System: When battery is low (or on voice command “return”), async drones:

  1. Navigate toward the operator’s GPS coordinates (broadcast by backpack)
  2. Switch to downward-facing camera
  3. Hailo-8 AI on backpack detects open palm gesture in the camera feed
  4. Drone descends and hovers ~10 cm above open palm
  5. Operator grabs it, or lets it gently land in hand

This means async drones never die mid-air from neglect — they always find their way home.

Activation: Press toggle button on top, open palm → drone takes off and awaits command.

Sync Drones (Suitcase, up to 12 units)#

Lightweight, vision-first drones designed for coordinated swarm behavior. All heavy AI computation runs centrally on the backpack, not on each drone.

Design philosophy (Tesla-style vision): Instead of loading each drone with expensive point sensors, sync drones use a single wide-angle camera and rely on the Hailo-8 AI chip on the backpack for depth estimation, obstacle avoidance, and formation positioning. This cuts cost by ~$250 per drone and significantly reduces weight, extending flight time.

Capabilities:

  • Wide-angle camera (OV2640, 160° FOV) for vision + AI depth estimation
  • GPS for formation positioning
  • 8× addressable RGB LEDs (WS2812B) for light shows, formation markers, identification
  • LoRa for receiving formation commands from backpack
  • USB-C for suitcase charging and calibration
  • WiFi CSI sensing (ESP32-S3) — distributed sensing mesh across 12 drones covers large areas
  • Surveillance coverage — overhead or multi-angle tracking, crowd counting, perimeter breach detection

Use cases:

  • Flight formations (circling a perimeter, geometric patterns, synchronized movement)
  • Light shows and LED choreography for events
  • Multi-angle filming — surround a subject from multiple simultaneous viewpoints
  • Podcasting/event coverage from overhead or multiple directions when outdoors
  • Relay networks — extend communication range across large areas
  • Group reconnaissance — cover a large area simultaneously with multiple viewpoints
  • Perimeter sensing mesh — station sync drones around a building; backpack detects presence/movement inside using CSI data from all nodes simultaneously

Suitcase Auto-Dock System: When battery is low (or on command), sync drones:

  1. Receive suitcase GPS coordinates broadcast by suitcase MCU over LoRa
  2. Navigate to suitcase location (works even if suitcase has moved)
  3. Switch to IR beacon homing for final approach — each bay emits a unique modulated IR frequency
  4. Magnetic pogo-pin connectors in the bay capture and seat the drone precisely
  5. Charging begins automatically; no manual plug-in needed

WiFi CSI Through-Wall Sensing#

What It Actually Is#

This is not X-ray vision. WiFi CSI (Channel State Information) sensing works by analyzing how WiFi signals are disturbed as they pass through walls and bounce off moving objects. Every person in a room distorts the signal slightly — you can read those distortions to infer what’s happening on the other side of a wall.

The video you referenced almost certainly covers either the ESP32-based CSI implementation or research like CMU’s “DensePose from WiFi” work. Both use this same principle: passive or active 2.4 GHz WiFi signals + signal processing + ML = through-wall sensing without any camera. ~Claude

What It Can Detect#

Capability Achievable Notes
Human presence (is anyone in there?) ✅ Yes Works reliably through drywall
Motion detection (are they moving?) ✅ Yes Very sensitive — even breathing
Breathing rate ✅ Yes Non-contact vital sign monitoring
Activity class (walking / sitting / falling) ✅ Yes CNN trained on CSI spectrograms
Rough location (which zone of the room) ✅ Yes Multiple nodes improve accuracy
Body pose / skeleton estimation ⚠️ Experimental Requires 3–6 nodes + ML training per environment
Number of people ⚠️ Partial Reliable up to ~3–5 people per node
Detailed image / video ❌ No CSI does not produce images — ever
Through thick concrete / metal walls ❌ Degraded 3–8 m max vs 5–15 m through drywall

Hardware: ESP32-S3 Per Drone#

The ESP32-S3 is the key chip. Espressif’s esp-csi firmware exposes raw CSI data (per-subcarrier amplitude and phase on 56 sub-carriers at 20 Hz). Combine with a directional 2.4 GHz antenna and the range extends significantly.

Why ESP32-S3 specifically: The original ESP32-C3 and single-core variants lack the processing headroom for real-time CSI DSP. The S3’s dual-core 240 MHz + AI instruction set is the minimum viable platform. The backpack RPi handles all the heavy ML inference.

Sensing modes on the drone:

  1. Active mode (drone transmits + receives its own reflected signals): Single-drone sensing. Best for detecting humans 3–8 m ahead through walls. The drone hovers outside a window or next to a wall; the ESP32-S3 pings itself on 2.4 GHz and reads the CSI of the return.

  2. Passive mode (drone receives signals from ambient WiFi — routers, phones, etc.): No transmission needed. Any 2.4 GHz traffic in the environment becomes a sensing source. Useful for detecting presence in buildings that already have WiFi.

  3. Multi-drone mesh mode (sync drones as distributed nodes): The most powerful configuration. Station multiple sync drones around a building — one transmits, others receive. Multi-static sensing with overlapping signal paths gives 360° room coverage. Think of it as a distributed radar made from your own drones. CSI data from all nodes is streamed back to the backpack RPi via LoRa (compressed, burst packets between flight commands) and processed centrally on the Hailo-8.

Range and Wall Penetration#

Wall Type Detection Range Notes
Drywall / plasterboard 5–15 m 2.4 GHz penetrates well
Brick 3–10 m Moderate attenuation
Reinforced concrete 3–8 m Sensitivity reduced; presence still detectable
Metal / foil insulation ~0 Blocks signal almost completely
Glass 10–18 m Near-transparent to 2.4 GHz

A research setup at 18.5 m across five rooms through multiple walls demonstrated reliable activity recognition with a single ESP32-S3 + directional biquad antenna. That’s your realistic ceiling. Typical useful range for strong detection: 5–10 m through a standard interior wall.

Integration Into the Drone System#

  • Async drone: Hover outside a target building. Run active CSI sensing for 10–30 seconds. Stream compressed CSI frames to backpack via LoRa. Hailo-8 runs the inference model and sends a presence/activity report to HMD.
  • Sync drone fleet: Perimeter deployment — distribute 4–8 sync drones around a building at different heights and angles. The multi-static geometry dramatically improves spatial resolution. Backpack fuses all 8 CSI streams into a room-level occupancy map shown in HMD.
  • Voice command: “Scan the building” + pointing gesture → async drone navigates to the indicated structure → hovers → runs CSI sweep → reports back.

Firmware / Software Stack#

  • Drone side: ESP32-S3 running esp-csi firmware (Espressif open source). Compressed CSI packet → sent to backpack over LoRa as burst alongside normal telemetry.
  • Backpack side: Python/Rust CSI processor on RPi 5. ML model (CNN on CSI spectrograms) running on Hailo-8. Options: RuView (WiFi DensePose, open source), ESPectre (simpler, presence only), or custom model trained on your target environment.
  • Training: Presence detection and fall detection work out of the box. Body pose estimation requires 10–15 minutes of environment-specific training per deployment location. Not plug-and-play for pose — but presence and motion are.

Hard Limits to Understand#

  1. You cannot image through walls — no photos, no video, ever. CSI is a signal distortion metric, not a camera.
  2. Static people are harder — if someone is sitting completely still, breathing detection still works but activity recognition accuracy drops.
  3. The ML model is environment-specific — furniture layout affects multipath. A model trained in one building may need retraining for a different room. MERIDIAN in RuView addresses this partially.
  4. Multiple simultaneous transmitters interfere — in multi-drone active mode, drones need to time-slice transmissions (managed by backpack LoRa scheduling).
  5. 2.4 GHz only — 5 GHz WiFi doesn’t penetrate walls well enough for this. All CSI sensing uses the 2.4 GHz band.

Solar Power System#

Philosophy#

~~The drones themselves are too small and power-hungry during flight for onboard solar to meaningfully contribute. A 3"-class drone consumes 30–50W in flight; a palm-sized solar cell produces ~0.1–0.5W. So solar on drones only charges them when docked and stationary in direct sun.

The real solar payoff is on the suitcase and backpack — larger surface area, lower continuous power draw. A 50W suitcase array in full sun can recharge 2–3 sync drone batteries per hour, or run the backpack RPi indefinitely.

Suitcase Solar Array (Primary Solar System)#

The suitcase lid and sides are the largest available surface area in the system. The design uses 4 modular 12.5W flexible solar panels (50W total) that fold flat for transport and deploy like wings. Each panel is mounted on a motorized arm.

Sun-tracking sub-system:

  • 4× LDR light sensors arranged in a quad (top/bottom/left/right of each panel) detect the brightest direction
  • A microcontroller reads differential LDR readings and drives servos to rotate each panel toward maximum irradiance
  • 2 SG90 servos per panel (azimuth + elevation tilt) = 8 servos total
  • This is genuinely a project within a project — but the PCA9685 servo driver controlled by the suitcase MCU (already present) handles all 16 PWM channels
  • In static deployment, sun-tracking adds ~20–30% more energy harvest vs a fixed-angle panel
  • During drone operations, panels fold flat (one button or voice command: “deploy solar”) and the tracking logic runs continuously. or simply a switch.

Swappable panel design: Each panel module connects via XT30 connectors and a mechanical bayonet mount. If one is damaged or dirty, swap it in seconds. Spare panels can be stored in the suitcase lid organizer.

Power routing: Suitcase solar → 4S MPPT charge controller → suitcase 4S LiPo → charges sync drone batteries. In field ops, this means continuous solar trickle charging extends the effective battery reserve beyond the 2 full cycles the battery alone provides.

Backpack Solar (Secondary)#

A 30W foldable monocrystalline panel (Renogy/Jackery style, ~2.5 kg, folds to 25×15 cm) attaches to the back of the backpack via loop fasteners. The operator is usually stationary or walking slowly during swarm ops — the panel faces the sun passably well.

  • Backpack MPPT controller handles charging of the 4S 20,000 mAh LiPo
  • 30W in direct sun = ~2A into 4S battery ≈ ~2.5 hrs to recover 10,000 mAh of the 20,000 mAh pack
  • This doesn’t replace the battery but meaningfully extends all-day operations

Belt Solar (Trickle Charging)#

~Two 100mm × 50mm flexible monocrystalline strips (0.5W each, 1W total) bond to the top surface of the belt drone bays. When async drones are docked and not flying, direct sun slowly trickles charge into docked batteries.

~At 1W for 4 drones sharing the power, the impact during active operations is minimal (0.25W per drone). This is more meaningful during standby — if the belt and drones are in sunlight for 2 hours, the 4 docked batteries can recover 10–15% charge passively.

Drone Solar (Optional / Experimental)#

~A single 40×40mm flexible monocrystalline cell (100mW) can be bonded to the top of each drone frame. At 2g, the weight penalty is acceptable for async drones but adds up on sync drones where weight is the core design constraint.

~During flight: negligible impact (0.2% of consumption). During docked charging in sun: modest contribution. Recommended for async drones if you want the experiment; skip on sync drones to protect flight time.

Surveillance Capabilities#

Overview#

Both drone types are surveillance platforms by hardware. The async drone’s sensor suite (dual-mode camera, thermal, IR illumination, GPS) makes it a powerful mobile evidence-gathering and tracking system. The sync fleet’s multi-angle coverage turns 12 simultaneous viewpoints into a distributed surveillance network.

Key principle: The Hailo-8 on the backpack already runs real-time AI inference. Surveillance AI models run on the same chip with no additional hardware — this is all software.

Async Drone — Surveillance Feature Set#

  • Face detection and tracking: Hailo-8 runs a real-time face detection model on the async camera feed. Lock onto a subject and the drone maintains framing automatically (follow-target mode).
  • Person re-identification: If a subject walks out of one drone’s view, the Hailo-8 model matches appearance features to re-identify them in another drone’s feed or after the drone repositions.
  • License plate recognition: The dual-mode camera in daylight mode has enough resolution for plate reads at 3–6 m range. An LPR model on the Hailo-8 can extract and log plate strings in real time.
  • Night surveillance: IR LED illumination + IR-cut filter removed = effective night-vision surveillance camera that is invisible to the naked eye. Thermal camera (optional) adds heat-signature detection for complete darkness.
  • Geo-tagged evidence logging: Every frame/clip logged with GPS coordinates, timestamp, drone ID, and battery state. Stored on NVMe SSD on the backpack RPi. Useful for forensics, journalism, security documentation.
  • Fall/incident detection: Hailo-8 person detection + pose estimation can flag when someone falls or is motionless — relevant for event security or search-and-rescue.

Sync Drone Fleet — Surveillance Feature Set#

  • Crowd counting: Multiple overhead camera angles fed to Hailo-8. Crowd density estimation across a large area.
  • Perimeter breach detection: Station sync drones around a perimeter. Any object crossing a defined boundary triggers an alert in the HMD.
  • Multi-angle subject lock: Surround a subject with 4–6 sync drones at different angles simultaneously. Impossible to escape the camera coverage without going indoors.
  • Formation surveillance sweep: Program the swarm to fly a search pattern across a large area (field, park, event space). Each drone streams its feed; Hailo-8 flags any detected persons.
  • WiFi CSI perimeter sensing: As described in the WiFi Sensing section — sync drones as distributed CSI nodes detect movement inside buildings without any cameras.

Surveillance Modes (Voice-Activated)#

  • “Track that person” + HMD reticle lock → face/body lock, drone follows
  • “Cover the perimeter” → sync fleet takes perimeter stations, breach detection active
  • “Sweep the area” → swarm runs grid search pattern
  • “Record and tag” → geo-tagged clip begins recording on current drone feed
  • “Night mode” → async drone switches to IR camera, activates IR illumination (will work automatically when LDR detects little to no light but good to be able to have a command for edge cases)

Storage for Evidence#

A 1 TB NVMe SSD on the backpack RPi stores:

  • Continuous compressed video from all active drone feeds
  • Face detection thumbnails with GPS + timestamp metadata
  • CSI sensing logs
  • System telemetry

At 8 Mbit/s per drone feed × 4 async + 12 sync = ~256 Mbit/s uncompressed. With H.264/H.265 compression at 10:1 ratio, 16 simultaneous feeds ≈ ~25 MB/s. A 1 TB drive gives roughly 11 hours of full-swarm recording.

  • Flight profile

No Bluetooth pairing required. Fast, reliable, repeatable.

Hardware Systems#

Computer Backpack#

Core of the entire system. Worn as a backplate or small backpack.

Components:

  • Raspberry Pi 5 (8 GB) — main computer
  • Hailo-8 AI HAT — 26 TOPS for real-time inference (face detection, object detection, depth estimation, palm recognition, voice processing, LPR, person re-ID)
  • ReSpeaker Mic Array v2.0 — far-field voice pickup (outdoor wind rejection)
  • 4S LiPo 20,000 mAh or 18650 cell pack — main power (4–8 hrs runtime)
  • Ebyte E22-900M LoRa module — master comms node for all drones
  • 10-port USB-C hub — belt (4 ports), suitcase tether (1), HMD, gloves, spare
  • Active cooling for RPi 5 (mandatory under sustained AI load)
  • 1 TB NVMe SSD — surveillance recording storage
  • 30W foldable solar panel + MPPT controller — field endurance extension

Drone Belt (4-bay, Async)#

Wearable belt with 2 bays per side. USB-C charges and calibrates all 4 async drones simultaneously. Battery fuel gauge ICs (MAX17048) report each drone’s charge status to the backpack. 1W flexible solar strip on top surface trickle-charges docked drones in sunlight.

Drone Suitcase (12-bay, Sync)#

Hard-shell Pelican-style case with:

  • 12 magnetic pogo-pin docking bays with TPU cradles
  • Modulated IR landing beacons per bay (unique frequency per slot)
  • USB calibration hub (all 12 drones configured simultaneously)
  • Battery fuel gauges and status display in lid
  • GPS module + LoRa module (broadcasts suitcase position to returning drones)
  • Power supply: Meanwell 12V 20A for indoor; 4S 20Ah LiPo for field (~2 full charge cycles for all 12)
  • 4× modular 12.5W flexible solar panels with motorized sun-tracking arms (50W total)
  • MPPT charge controller for field solar harvesting
  • 8× SG90 servos + PCA9685 driver for solar panel tracking
  • Quad-LDR sun direction sensor array

Head-Mounted Display#

Path A — Realistic: Meta Quest 3#

Buy a Quest 3 and build a custom app. Passthrough AR lets you see surroundings while overlaying drone feeds. Connect to backpack RPi via USB-C link cable (lowest latency) or wirelessly via Virtual Desktop. App shows drone feeds, formation controls, voice command shortcuts, hand-gesture buttons, CSI sensing occupancy map, surveillance alerts, geo-tagged evidence log, and solar power status.

Path B — Delusional: Custom Smart Glasses#

Build everything from scratch:

  • FPGA (Lattice ECP5 or Xilinx Artix-7) for parallel graphics rendering and sensor processing — MCUs are too slow for this
  • Micro OLED displays (Sony ECX339A or eMagin) — one per eye, need beam-splitter or prism optics to overlay on real vision
  • Waveguide / prism optics — the hardest and most expensive part; diffractive waveguides (Lumus, DigiLens) require high MOQ, prism optics are cheaper but bulkier
  • 4 cameras (OV5640): 2 front-facing, 2 rear (in arm tips) for full 360° awareness
  • Optional retina scanner for login security
  • MEMS microphones with DSP for outdoor noise cancellation
  • Custom titanium or carbon fiber frame

This path is far more capable if completed but is a multi-year engineering project in itself.

Motion Gloves#

One pair. Used for precision control of any individual drone.

Activation: Open-hand-from-fist gesture → system confirms “precision gloves enabled” Pause: Double pointer-to-thumb tap → drone holds position

Components per glove:

  • nRF52840 MCU (BLE built-in, ultra-low power)
  • ICM-42688-P IMU for wrist orientation (roll/pitch/yaw)
  • 5× Spectra Symbol flex sensors (one per finger) for fine control
  • LiPo 150–200 mAh in wrist band
  • ERM haptic motor for feedback (command confirmed, low battery alert, palm-return acknowledged)

Battery Strategy#

Async Drones#

  • Expected flight time: 15–25 min per charge (full sensor payload)
  • Strategy: 3 batteries per drone, hot-swapped on belt
  • 4 drones × 3 batteries = ~45–75 min continuous coverage via rotation
  • Palm auto-return ensures drones never die mid-air
  • Belt solar adds trickle charge in sunlight when docked

Sync Drones#

  • Expected flight time: 25–35 min per charge (lighter, fewer sensors)
  • Strategy: 2 batteries per drone; charge in suitcase between sets
  • Plan light show / formation sets in 20-min blocks
  • Auto-dock in suitcase on low battery
  • Suitcase solar extends field endurance significantly: 50W in full sun recharges ~2–3 batteries/hour

Backpack#

  • 20,000 mAh 4S LiPo: 4–8 hrs RPi5 runtime plus drone charging
  • 30W solar panel adds ~2A in direct sun — meaningful for all-day operations
  • Use 18650 cell pack with proper BMS for wearable safety (no puffing risk)

Future Ideas#

  • Nuclear micro-batteries — when/if commercially available, flight times become irrelevant; until then, rotation is the strategy
  • Solid-state LiPo — projected to push flight times toward 40–45 min at this scale, possibly by ~2030
  • SDR per async drone — passive RF listening/research; strictly receive-only (jamming is illegal everywhere)
  • On-drone AI (async) — if battery tech improves enough, each async drone could carry its own inference chip instead of relying on backpack; gives true independence
  • Suitcase auto-landing with moving suitcase — suitcase broadcasts GPS continuously, drones always have a live target even if you’re walking with it
  • UWB radar addition — ultra-wideband radar (Novelda XeThru or Acconeer A111) gives higher-resolution through-wall sensing than WiFi CSI at the cost of a dedicated radio. Could augment ESP32-S3 CSI on the async drone for confirmed presence + breathing even in metal-heavy environments. (good reason for SDR)
  • Perovskite solar cells — when available, 30–40% efficiency vs 20–22% for current monocrystalline. Would nearly double the solar harvest from the same panel area on the suitcase.
  • IEEE 802.11bf (WiFi SENS standard) — finalized in 2024, this standard formally defines WiFi sensing. Future WiFi chips will expose CSI natively as a standard feature, improving hardware availability and driver support.
