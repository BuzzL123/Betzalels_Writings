Isn’t That Offensive?

2026-04-07
archiveimported

Before wrapping up, let’s address the term head-on. When I describe certain strands of modern leftist idealism as an “infectious disease,” I’m not reaching for shock value or cheap rhetoric. I’m using the word in its precise, dictionary sense:

disease n. 2. a harmful development (as in a social institution) Example: sees the city’s crime as a disease.

infectious adj. 2. spreading or capable of spreading rapidly to others - an infectious laugh - Her happiness was infectious. — Merriam-Webster Dictionary https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infectious

Another word I’ll be using throughout this essay:

virus n. 2. something that poisons the mind or soul - “… the force of this virus of prejudice …” — V. S. Waters — Merriam-Webster Dictionary https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/virus

Just as a biological virus hijacks cells to replicate itself, certain ideas — particularly those that prioritize raw empathy and utopian ideals over observable reality — can spread rapidly through social networks, institutions, and minds. This is infective thinking: it replicates fast and feels compassionate, yet leads to stagnation, inversion of reality, and societal harm. Effective thinking, by contrast, anchors in evidence, biology, tradition, and outcomes that actually progress humanity. The dictionary backs the metaphor; the evidence in the preceding sections backs its application.

Has It Always Been Like This?#

Old-school liberalism once meant something clear and effective: individual rights, reason over emotion, free speech, limited government, equality under the law, and merit-based progress. Classical liberals fought for the Enlightenment values that built the modern world — evidence, personal liberty, and institutions that rewarded competence.

What we see today is not that liberalism. It has mutated into something else entirely: identity-first, emotion-driven, and obsessed with tearing down the very structures that once protected freedom. The disease did not appear overnight. It has ancient roots, but the modern strain took hold in the 20th century. Understanding the old version shows just how far the current one has strayed.

Claiming Progressivism but Delivering Stagnation#

The label says “progress,” but the results show the opposite. Institutions captured by infective thinking deliver decline — not advancement — in every measurable way.

This Isn’t Our First Rodeo – Weimar and Nazi Germany Parallels#

History offers a warning. Weimar Germany in the 1920s looked eerily familiar: economic chaos, cultural degeneracy, hyper-sexualization, and a collapsing trust in institutions. The old liberal order fractured under the weight of its own excesses. What followed was not a return to sanity but a far darker authoritarian backlash.

If this ideology continues unchecked, the next chapter is predictable: deepening division, institutional collapse, and conflicts that pit the infective mindset against any remaining effective structures. We are not doomed to repeat Weimar, but we are repeating the conditions that made it possible.

This Is Only the First Level#

This essay diagnoses the disease — the infective thinking that hijacks minds, inverts reality, and stagnates progress. But it is only the first level. The next essay examines moral inversion and normalization of the unnormalized. See the related essay “Everyone’s Depressed” for how it hijacks empathy and identity at the personal level.
